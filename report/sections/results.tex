\section{Results and analyses}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Baseline verification of the homeostatic plasticity rule}
\label{sec:results:substrate}

One hundred random five-node CTRNNs were instantiated with parameters sampled
from the evolution ranges (Table~\ref{tab:param-ranges}) and run through three
phases: $220$ timesteps with HP disabled (baseline), $6000$ timesteps with HP
active, and a further $220$ timesteps with HP disabled again. The fraction of
firing-rate samples falling outside $[H_L, H_U]$ was $0.861$ before HP, $0.532$
during HP, and $0.594$ after HP was switched off (Figure~\ref{fig:substrate}).
HP produces a substantial reduction in out-of-range activity, and the partial
persistence after HP is disabled confirms that the rule reshapes network
parameters rather than simply masking saturation while active. The residual gap
between the during-HP and after-HP values reflects the same HP-dependence that
the frozen-HP test (Section~\ref{sec:results:frozenhp}) probes in evolved
networks.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
   \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{../figs/substrate_check.pdf}
  \caption{Firing-rate distributions of 100 random CTRNNs before HP, during HP
  (final 220 steps), and after HP was switched off. Dashed lines mark
  $H_L = 0.2$ and $H_U = 0.8$.}
  \label{fig:substrate}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Replicating Williams's evolvability ordering}
\label{sec:results:replication}

Ten evolutionary runs per condition produced the fitness curves and final-fitness
distribution shown in Figure~\ref{fig:replication}. Table~\ref{tab:final-fitness}
summarises the final-generation statistics.

\begin{table}[t]
  \centering
  \caption{Final-generation best fitness across $n = 10$ runs per condition.}
  \label{tab:final-fitness}
  \begin{tabular}{lcc}
    \toprule
    Condition & Mean & SD \\
    \midrule
    No HP           & 0.674 & 0.096 \\
    Dev only        & 0.838 & 0.059 \\
    Behaviour only  & 0.709 & 0.097 \\
    Both            & 0.675 & 0.077 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

A Kruskal--Wallis test across the four conditions gives $H = 17.71$,
$p = 0.0005$. Pairwise Mann--Whitney $U$ tests with Bonferroni correction (six
pairs) place \emph{Dev only} significantly above each of the other three
conditions: vs.\ \emph{No HP} $p_{\text{bonf}} = 0.0035$, vs.\ \emph{Behaviour
only} $p_{\text{bonf}} = 0.0217$, vs.\ \emph{Both} $p_{\text{bonf}} = 0.0035$.
\emph{No HP}, \emph{Behaviour only} and \emph{Both} are mutually
indistinguishable (all pairwise $p_{\text{bonf}} = 1.000$).

The qualitative ordering reproduces Williams's: a developmental HP phase before
evaluation produces the best evolved fitness. The departure from Williams is
that \emph{Behaviour only} and \emph{Both} are not statistically worse than
\emph{No HP} in our data, whereas Williams reports them as worse; the
implications of this divergence are taken up in
Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsection{Search replication: early progress and run consistency}
\label{sec:results:searchreplication}

Williams reports two qualitative search-level observations about plastic
networks: faster early progress and greater run-to-run consistency. Both are
visible in Figure~\ref{fig:replication}. \emph{Dev only} reaches a clearly
higher mean fitness within the first ten generations than any of the other
three conditions and remains the most consistent across runs (the smallest
final-fitness SD, $0.059$). The other three conditions improve gradually across
all $200$ generations and show comparable run-to-run spread. The pattern that networks with a developmental HP phase have an early advantage
over non-plastic networks is reproduced; the further claim, that online HP also
accelerates early progress, is not, and tracks the non-significance of the
online-HP conditions in the final-fitness comparison.

\subsection{Behavioural trajectories}
\label{sec:results:trajectories}

To see what the evolved controllers actually do, the best individual
from each condition was replayed across three shared-seed trials with
per-timestep recording of agent position, shape position, and neural firing
rates (Figure~\ref{fig:trajectories}). Firing-rate cells are coloured by
viable-range state: blue below $H_L$, off-white within $[H_L, H_U]$, red above
$H_U$. All four representative individuals track the falling shapes
competently; the differences are in the underlying neural activity.

The \emph{No HP} individual operates with all neurons at saturation for nearly
the whole trial: each neuron remains at either its upper or lower rail
throughout. The \emph{Dev only} individual is evaluated with HP frozen, as is
the case for all Dev only trials, but the developmental phase has left the
network with more dynamic firing patterns: neurons alternate between above-range
and below-range states rather than remaining saturated. The \emph{Behaviour
only} individual operates largely within the viable range, with one sensor neuron
showing the same alternating pattern; \emph{Both} shows broader bands and more
in-range time than \emph{No HP}.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
   \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../figs/behavioural_trajectories.pdf}
  \caption{Behavioural trajectories for the best individual per condition,
  three shared-seed trials each. Top of each panel: agent and shape
  $x$-positions over time. Bottom: five-neuron firing-rate heatmap; blue
  $z < H_L$, off-white $z \in [H_L, H_U]$, red $z > H_U$.}
  \label{fig:trajectories}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Viable-range diagnostics across evolution}
\label{sec:results:viablerange}

The qualitative differences in Section~\ref{sec:results:trajectories} are
quantified at population level using the $\mathrm{frac}_V$ and entry--exit rate
metrics defined in Section~\ref{sec:methods:analysis}, computed at sampled
generations in both HP-active and HP-frozen modes.

Final-generation means across runs and neurons
(Figure~\ref{fig:viablerange}) show that with HP active, all three HP conditions
achieve $\mathrm{frac}_V$ between $0.43$ and $0.45$, well above the \emph{No HP}
baseline of $0.19$. With HP frozen, \emph{Dev only} and \emph{Behaviour only}
collapse to $\mathrm{frac}_V \approx 0.08$, at or below the \emph{No HP}
baseline; \emph{Both} retains slightly more activity in range ($0.16$). The
entry--exit rate at the final generation is elevated in all three HP conditions
relative to \emph{No HP} ($1.49$ transitions per $1000$ steps): \emph{Dev only}
$9.55$, \emph{Behaviour only} $5.89$, \emph{Both} $4.62$. When HP is frozen,
all three HP conditions show rates near zero ($0.04$, $0.05$, $0.02$
respectively), collapsing to or below the \emph{No HP} level. The pattern is
consistent across conditions: viable-range operation and the dynamic firing
patterns associated with it are held in place by HP and collapse when HP is
removed.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
   \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../figs/viable_range_compact.pdf}
  \caption{Per-neuron viable-range diagnostics, mean across runs and neurons
  at the final generation. Left: $\mathrm{frac}_V$ with HP active and with
  HP frozen. Right: final-generation entry--exit rate per $1000$ timesteps,

  \label{fig:viablerange}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Frozen-HP test: has assimilation occurred?}
\label{sec:results:frozenhp}

The central extension is the frozen-HP test (Section~\ref{sec:methods:analysis}).
Figure~\ref{fig:frozenhp} shows HP-active fitness against frozen fitness for all
40 final-generation best individuals. The bottom panel shows the raw fitness drop
(HP-active minus frozen) for each individual.

\emph{No HP} individuals show negligible drops, consistent with HP having played
no role in their training. The HP conditions show a clear departure from this
baseline. For \emph{Dev only}, where the frozen evaluation removes the
developmental phase entirely and runs the individual straight from its genotype,
fitness drops range from $0.56$ to $0.83$ in absolute terms, with $5/10$
individuals collapsing to zero fitness. For \emph{Behaviour only}, drops range
from near zero to $0.83$, with $3/10$ complete or near-complete collapses. For
\emph{Both}, the pattern is bimodal: $3/10$ complete collapses and $4/10$
moderate drops.

The finding is consistent across all three HP conditions: the behaviour is
HP-dependent. In a substantial fraction of individuals HP is not merely
beneficial but constitutive --- removing it collapses the behaviour entirely.
Genetic assimilation, which would predict small or zero drops, is not observed.

  \caption{Frozen-HP test. Top: HP-active fitness against frozen-HP fitness for

\subsection{Search dynamics of the larger adaptive system}
\label{sec:results:searchdynamics}

The analyses above examine the evolved controller. The fourth analysis examines
the search itself. Per-generation population best, mean, and fitness spread were
computed from the full saved population fitness arrays
(Figure~\ref{fig:searchdynamics}). The conditions show two distinct patterns.
\emph{Behaviour only} and \emph{Both} converge rapidly: population fitness
spread drops below $0.02$ by generation $3$--$4$ and remains low throughout.
\emph{No HP} and \emph{Dev only} maintain substantially higher spread across all
$200$ generations (final spread $\approx 0.11$ and $0.12$ respectively),
consistent with continued population-level diversity rather than convergence to
a single region. Despite this difference, fitness continues to improve in all
four conditions across the full run. The most notable feature is the \emph{Dev
only} starting point: at generation $0$, before any selection, its mean best
fitness is already $0.619$, substantially above the other three conditions
($0.508$--$0.525$) --- a direct consequence of the developmental phase running
before the very first evaluation.

  \centering
   \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../figs/search_dynamics_population.pdf}
  \caption{Population-level search dynamics, one panel per condition. Solid
  line: mean population best fitness. Dashed line: mean population mean fitness.
  Dotted line: mean population fitness SD across $n = 10$ runs (a diversity
  proxy).}
  \label{fig:searchdynamics}
\end{figure}
