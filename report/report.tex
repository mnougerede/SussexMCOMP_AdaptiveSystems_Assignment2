\begin{abstract}
Homeostatic plasticity (HP) improves continuous-time recurrent neural networks
as a behavioural substrate, yet its effect on evolvability is mixed: a
developmental phase of HP before evaluation aids evolution while online HP does
not. We replicate Williams's (2006) ball-catching evolvability experiment across
four HP conditions and extend it with a frozen-HP test that asks, in
Baldwin-effect terms, whether behaviour evolved under HP has been genetically
assimilated. It has not. Removing HP from evolved controllers by adiabatic
elimination drops fitness far beyond the measurement-noise baseline and
collapses behaviour entirely in a substantial fraction of individuals across all
HP conditions. The best-performing condition, developmental HP, is also the most
HP-dependent --- a paradox explained by a selection regime that never exposed
the genotype's un-developed competence to evolution. HP here is constitutive of
behaviour rather than assimilated into the genotype.
\end{abstract}
